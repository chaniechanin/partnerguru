01 · A Point of View
01

A point of view

Most partnership problems aren't really about the business.

They're about people. Two capable, well-meaning partners who once shared a vision — and somewhere along the way started seeing the future differently. The disagreement looks like it's about money, or control, or a clause in a contract. Underneath, it's almost always about trust, fairness, and expectations that were never said out loud.

By the time most people reach for a lawyer, the expensive part has already begun — not just in legal fees, but in time, focus, and relationships that took years to build. A problem a few honest conversations could have solved becomes a fight no one really wins.

I've spent more than 25 years on the other side of that pattern: helping partners slow down, get to the truth of what's actually going on, and make the decision that protects what matters most. Sometimes that means realigning and moving forward together. Sometimes it means separating with respect. Either way, it means deciding with clarity instead of fear.

The cost of the wrong decision is always higher than the cost of clarity.

04 · A Situation I See Often
Illustrative example · details changed

When “buy me out” wasn't really the problem.

Two partners had built a distribution business over fifteen years — worth several million, and the backbone of both their families. Then one of them wanted out. He demanded a buyout and hinted at lawyers. The other felt blindsided and betrayed. Within weeks they were barely speaking, and each had a litigator's number on the desk.

In our confidential sessions, a different story surfaced. The partner who wanted out had had a health scare. He wasn't chasing money — he was chasing security, and didn't know how to say so. The other wasn't refusing to be fair — he felt abandoned by the person he'd trusted for half his life.

Once that was on the table, the real question changed. Not “who wins the buyout,” but “how do we honor both of their futures?” We structured a gradual, partial buyout with an earnout: one partner stepped back with the security he needed, while the other took majority control and kept building.

05 · Before You Litigate

A word from experience

Even considering litigation? Talk to me first.

I'm not an attorney, and I won't represent you in court. But I've been through litigation myself — and I've guided others through serious disputes for over 25 years. I know what a legal battle really costs: in money, in time, and in relationships that can't be rebuilt.

Sometimes litigation is the right call. More often, there's a faster, less damaging path. Either way, you'll see it clearly before you commit.
